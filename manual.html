2. Train Driving Basics

Typically a train consists of 5 wagons, which can total up to over 167,000 kg in mass. Keep the train length and in mass when driving it. A train possesses considerable inertia even when empty.

The subway driver must pay attention and act according to track signals, track signs and dispatcher instructions to ensure efficient and safe operation of the train.

The trains use rheostat control for starting and braking (Em, Ema508), thyristor control for braking (81-717, Ema509). It takes some time for rheostat to spin into the right position. Try to get a good feeling of this delay.

To avoid damage to the engines and discomfort of the passengers, the current through engines is limited. Effective acceleration and braking of train is 1.5 m/s2.

Electric System

The train electric circuits are divided into auxiliary circuits, power circuits and control circuits. There are four electric engines, two engines in each bogey - connected in series (engines #1 and #3, engines #2 and #4). The two pairs of engines can be in turn connected in series or in parallel.

Each engine consists of an anchor (a rotor) and a stator. All subway trains described in this manual use series excitation of engine. This means that current flows through anchor, then flows through stator.

Current through stator will create a magnetic field, in which the anchor will rotate. The rotation of anchor generates current in opposite of current which accelerates it. When current that flows through the engine equals counter-current generated by the engines, train stops accelerating. This is known as automatic mode.

During the initial start of the train, there is no magnetic field available and so no counter-current is generated. If engines would be connected in series to the 750 volts power source, the total current flowing through them would be (750 volts) / (4 * 0.0691 ohm) = 2710 A. Such current would destroy the engines.

To limit the current flowing through engines during startup a rheostat is used. It adds additional resistance to the power circuits, limiting the current to 240-260 A for an empty train, and up to 310-340 A on slopes and under load

Acceleration of the train depends on current through the anchor. Rotation rate is dependant on current through the stator (which is usually equal to current through the anchor).

Control System

When trains are coupled together mechanically, the electric ports located on the coupling are connected too. Each of the pins creates a train wire, single conductor which is shared by all the wagons of the train.

Train wires are used to transmit control signals to all of the wagons of the train.

Sbor - assembly of the power circuits, all the relays close and create a specific circuit for braking or acceleration. Razbor - opposite process, circuits are disconnected and disassembled by opening relays and contactors.

Pneumatic System

Pressure in brake cylinders is defined by difference of pressure between brake line and train line/reservoir. To increase pressure in brake cylinder, brake line pressure must be released.

The pneumatic system is shared across all of the trains wagons. This is controlled by the isolation valves. These valves allow connection of brake line and train line through the coupling.

Make sure that isolation valves on both ends of the train are CLOSED, while all the isolation valves between wagons are OPEN. Closed valves will prevent operation of the pneumatic system across entire train and will decrease braking efficiency.

Train Controls

The main instrument of a driver is the controller handle. It controls the way in which electric circuits of the train will be connected. The second instrument is the drivers valve, which allows contorol over the pneumatic system of the train.

Direction of train movement is selected by the reverser. Reverser is an additional rotor which coincides with the controller and has three positions: forward, 0, backward.

Only one wagon in the train can have reverser set to forward. Right now you have to pay extra attention to make sure of that.

The controller occupies one of the following seven positions:

X3 Engines connected in parallel
X2 Engines connected in series
X1 Manuvering mode
0 All engines disconnected
T1 Manual braking (electric brakes engaged)
T1AManual braking (removes a step of resistance from the rheostat)
T2 Automatic braking/full stop mode

The drivers valve controls pneumatic system state and has five different positions:

1Charge (fast release) Brake line is filled from train line
2Driving (brake release)Brake line and reservoir filled from train line
3Closed All valves are closed, drivers valve is disabled (no flow)
4Service application Brake line is released to atmosphere, brake cylinders fed from train line
5Emergency application Fast application of brakes

Normal pneumatic braking involves moving drivers valve from position 2 to position 4 until desired pressure in brake line is reached. Full application of brakes equals to 3 atm of pressure in brake line. Releasing brakes is done by moving the valve to position 2 (driving).

The head train must have pneumatic valve in position 2 for normal driving to avoid overcharging. Intermediate and rear trains must all have driver valve disabled by setting it to position 3.

Do not leave drivers valve in the position 1 (charge) for longer than needed to avoid overcharging of the brake line.

3. Train Controller Modes

X1 (Manuvering)

To avoid overheating of the resistors, limit duration of operating in the X1 mode to no more than 3 minutes.

X2 (Series)

X3 (Parallel)

Once X3 mode is engaged, the train will stay in it even if controller is moved to X2 or X1.

T1 (Manual brake)

T1A (Manual brake, next rheostat position)

To remove a single position from rheostat and increase strength of brakes by one step, cycle controller between T1 and T1A. Repeat until desired braking efficiency is reached.

Typically you would brake to 20 kph by alternating between T1 and T1A, then engage T2 for full stop.

T2 (Automatic brake)

When rheostat reaches position 17, pneumatic valve #1 will be open and pneumatic brakes will be applied.

In the T2 mode, if electric brakes do not gain enough efficiency, they will be replaced by pneumatic braking.

4. Troubleshooting for Dummies

Train refuses to move

  1. Is one RP light glowing? Check every wagon until you find one with two RP lights glowing.
  2. Are two RP lights glowing? Press G to return overload relay.
  3. Are pneumatic brakes engaged? Release brakes by pressing R
  4. Is reverser set to neutral in all wagons? Reset reverser to neutral position in all wagons.
  5. Try cycling reverser between forward and backward position
  6. Try cycling controller between X1 and 0 positions with pneumatic brakes engaged

Pneumatic brakes do not engage or do not release

  1. Check all isolation valves.
  2. Make sure isolation valves on train ends are CLOSED
  3. OPEN isolation valves between trains. Make sure to open them on both ends of the coupling.

Electric brakes don't work or are not efficient

  1. Use pneumatic brakes
  2. Set controller to 0. Try to assemble brake circuit. Repeat a couple times.
  3. Cycle reverser between forward and reverse position a couple times

Train feels sluggish or slow, cannot climb hills

  1. Make an emergency stop and engage pneumatic brakes
  2. Do all things listed under 'Train refuses to move' problem
  3. Check all wheels and bogeys to make sure there is no derail
